For this project, I was involved early on in designing the research question. I drew on prior knowledge about the causes of populism to conduct a short literature review and wrote the motivation section for our project. Using this foundation, I proposed a preliminary research approach that aligned with the academic literature and helped ensure the project would meaningfully contribute to the field. I also wrote much of the data section and, working with others, selected variables that corresponded to the economic, political, and cultural dimensions typically associated with populist support.

On the technical side, I focused on exploring unsupervised learning approaches, as our group had divided the work into distinct areas. I experimented with K-means clustering and DBSCAN to investigate whether underlying groupings existed in the data, particularly whether certain clusters were associated with support for radical right-wing populist parties. The goal was to determine whether these clusters could be meaningfully interpreted in relation to demographic traits or political attitudes. However, despite testing several iterations (including clustering on economic, cultural, and political variables, and across countries), the results were not meaningful, and we ultimately chose not to include this analysis in the final report.

Later in the project, when the sections were combined, I reviewed the full report file. This involved editing parts of the code to ensure everything ran smoothly and that the outputs, especially graphs, were as expected. Initially, some visualisations were not displaying correctly, so I worked through the code to correct these issues. I also checked that for each model there was a justification and interpretation section. 

In terms of collaboration, I helped initiate the group chat and organise in-person meetings early on to establish communication and alignment. These initial meetings were useful in setting a shared vision for the project. Generally, we worked well together, staying in contact and helping each other when neccessary. We were also mindful of each team member’s availability and aimed to allocate work accordingly, something we managed well by coordinating around people’s other deadlines. Since my clustering analysis was not ultimately used, I offered support on the CatBoost modelling, reviewing outputs and suggesting edits, including changes to evaluation metrics and interpretations.

This project taught me the value of balancing individual responsibility with collective coordination. Our consistent meetings ensured that even when working on different tasks, we stayed informed about the dataset and broader aims, making it easier for us to do individual work too. I also learned the importance of coherence: not all work needs to be included, and excluding redundant or inconclusive material can strengthen the overall narrative. Even when substantial effort goes into a method, it is often better to leave it out if it does not serve the core analysis.
